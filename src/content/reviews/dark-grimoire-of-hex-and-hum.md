They do not merely play; they become the labyrinth.

There is a particular silence that inhabits the space between die rolls — a held breath, a candle-flicker, the rustle of a hexmap being unfolded for the first time. Hex & Hum have spent three records trying to score that silence, and with *Dark Grimoire* they have finally succeeded in a way that feels less like an album and more like a companion spirit.

The record opens with "Cartomancer's Lament," a fourteen-minute piece built around a single motif on prepared piano and what sounds like a hurdy-gurdy filtered through four generations of magnetic tape degradation. It is not pleasant in any conventional sense. It is *correct*.

## The Sound of Mechanical Fate

What separates dungeon synth from mere ambient fantasy music is the sense that something is at stake. Hex & Hum understand this intuitively. Every track on *Dark Grimoire* feels like it was composed for a moment of consequence — not background, but foreground, draped over the silence of players thinking.

"Hexcrawl at Dusk" is the album's centerpiece and its thesis statement: twenty minutes of slowly evolving drone, punctuated by what sounds like dice rolling on a wooden table, the creak of a dungeon door, and a chorus of voices reciting something in a language that may or may not be real.

## Verdict

*Dark Grimoire of Hex & Hum* is the finest piece of tabletop-adjacent music released in years. It respects its listener's imagination enough to leave room in it.
